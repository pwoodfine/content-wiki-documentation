> An outbound email carries one subject line, a body organised around one idea per paragraph, and a sign-off — personal communication shaped by the tenant's voice, not marketing prose.

An **email** in the Foundry language-protocol stack belongs to
the COMMS family alongside the chat template. It represents
outbound correspondence: a message from a tenant user to a
colleague, client, or counterparty. The Doorman routes email
through a per-tenant adapter so the voice reflects the sender's
organisation, not a generic AI default. The machine-readable
counterpart lives in
`service-disclosure/templates/email.toml`.

## When to use this template

Use the email template when the Doorman is generating or
improving outbound correspondence. Common cases: a client update,
a request to a supplier, a follow-up after a meeting, a formal
notice that does not require a contract or legal instrument.

When a message is too long, too informal, or too urgent for
email — consider the chat template for the informal side and the
contract or CLA templates for formal legal instruments.
The email template occupies the space between: it is personal,
considered, and complete.

## Frontmatter fields

Emails are transient communications and do not carry YAML
frontmatter. The routing parameters that matter at request time:

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| `tenant` | Required. The tenant slug selects the per-tenant adapter that shapes voice and tone. |
| `genre_template` | `GenreTemplate::Email` |
| `register` | Defaults to `bloomberg` (the cross-platform base register). The per-tenant adapter may override for formal or informal contexts. |

The `tenant_required = true` flag means the Doorman will reject
an email request that does not name a tenant. Email is inherently
personal communication; a tenantless email has no voice.

## Structure

The template requires three parts:

| Part | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Subject** | One line. Specific and concrete — the reader should know what the email is about without opening it. No clickbait phrasing; no vague subjects like "Following up" or "Quick question". |
| **Body** | The message. One idea per paragraph. State the purpose in the first paragraph. Supporting context, details, and any action required follow in subsequent paragraphs. |
| **Sign-off** | The sender's name and role. No automated disclaimer unless a legal obligation requires one. |

The subject line is part of the template, not a metadata field.
It travels with the email body and is generated by the same
request.

## Register and tone

The register is Bloomberg-standard as the baseline: precise,
professional, and understandable to a financially literate reader
without a technical background. Sentence-length budget: mean
around fourteen words, maximum around twenty-two — shorter than
a TOPIC or ARCHITECTURE document because emails are read quickly.

The per-tenant adapter adjusts this baseline for the tenant's
context. A formal professional services firm sends more formal
email than a casual internal tool team. The Doorman applies the
adapter; contributors writing prompts or reviewing outputs check
against the tenant's established tone.

Active voice. Avoid hedging adverbs — "just", "simply",
"basically", "honestly" — that telegraph diffidence. An email
that says "I just wanted to follow up" communicates less
confidence than "I am following up". The difference is not
style; it is clarity.

The banned-vocabulary list applies. Email is personal
communication. Marketing-grade prose ("seamless experience",
"industry-leading solution") reads as a sales pitch, not a
letter from one person to another.

## Example

```
Subject: Contract review — agenda for Thursday

Hi Jennifer,

Attached is the draft contract for your review ahead of
Thursday's call. Sections 3 and 5 are the ones that need your
attention; the rest is standard form.

Please let me know by Wednesday noon if you have questions
you would like to address on the call.

Peter Woodfine
Operations
```
